🎲 Ludogogy

Ludogogy — Learning with Engagement

AI and Game Consultation. Making free learning fun again and helping organisations navigate the intersection of AI and education.

Ludogogy (from Latin ludus — play/game + Greek -gogy — leading/teaching) is Dr McCallum's consultancy specialising in gamification and AI interventions for organisations. With 25 years of experience in AI and game development, it bridges the gap between cutting-edge research and practical application.

Generative AI lets people produce human-like output with less effort — but this risks undermining genuine learning. Ludogogy helps organisations understand how AI can improve productivity while keeping learning meaningful and engaging.

⚔️ Experimental Archaeology

Traction Trebuchet Research

Nine years, thirteen builds: challenging assumptions about medieval siege weapons through hands-on experimental archaeology.

Challenging Conventional Wisdom

Common assumptions hold that traction trebuchets required extensive logistical support, trained crews, and expert craftsmen. Our research — published in the EXARC Journal of Experimental Archaeology — demonstrates that smaller traction trebuchets were in fact simple, rapidly constructed weapons that anyone with basic axe skills could build and operate.

Working with a team of computer science students, craftsmen, and volunteers, we constructed 13 traction trebuchets between 2008 and 2017 at university grounds, the Medieval Festival at Hamar (Norway), and Middelaldercenteret in Denmark — including one built entirely with period hand tools.

Historical Context

The first known European description of a traction trebuchet comes from Thessalonika in the late sixth century, where a raiding Avar army built siege engines on-site in just one to three days. In western Europe, the first probable description is from the Siege of Paris (885–886), where Frankish defenders built mangana in watchtowers to repel Viking attackers.

Medieval depictions in the Maciejowski Bible (mid-13th century) and building instructions by Al-Tarsusi show machines that are simpler than the later, more famous counterweight trebuchets. Our experiments confirm that these were rapid-fire anti-personnel weapons — a fundamentally different tactical role.

Key Findings

Build time: 12–30 hours depending on tools and crew experience. A team with basic carpentry skills could build one in under two days, consistent with historical accounts.

Rate of fire: Approximately 4 projectiles per minute — roughly 30 projectiles in the time needed to load and fire one shot from a small counterweight trebuchet.

Mobility: Completed machines could be moved by just 9 people (the operational crew plus two). They could be built out of range of fortification artillery, then quickly positioned where needed.

Reliability: Remarkably consistent throws within a 5m spread after initial adjustment. Green wood construction prevents catastrophic failure — machines slump rather than collapse when damaged.

Machine Components

Base Two long runners with vertical uprights, supported by triangular bracing. Strongest bracing on the back side where the crew pulls.
Axle Short rounded log (~150 cm long, ~10 cm diameter) spanning the uprights with a flat notch for the arm.
Throwing Arm The most critical component. Green wood, placed on top of (not through) the axle to retain full strength. 3–6.5 m length.
Arm Bracing Vertical ("axe-style") orientation aligned with stress direction, supporting the arm and wedging the axle in place.
Sling & Hook Simple leather sling with loop fitting onto a bendable metal release hook. Hook angle adjusts the release point for aiming.
Pulling Ropes Six ropes for a standard crew, expandable to twelve. All pullers pull inline with arm rotation — no twisting force.
Citation: Hjelsvold, S. & McCallum, S. (2018). "Traction Trebuchet." EXARC Journal, Issue 2018/3. Read the full article at exarc.net ↗
